## The One-Sentence Version

Four hundred years ago, the Admiralty engineered an entire civilization, called them "dragons," convinced a coastal town they were sacred protectors, and spent four centuries harvesting their biology — while the Foundry Collective discovered the secret eighty years ago, turned it into an arms economy, and both factions are now racing to either expose or preserve a lie that has consumed the last of the beings they were supposed to protect.

## Origin Story: The Dragon Compact and the Admiralty's Role

### What the Public Knows

The Dragon Compact was signed 400 years ago, when the first settlers of Coralshore found that the caves beneath the cliffs were inhabited. After a brief period of negotiation, an agreement was reached: the beings of the deep chambers (called "dragons" by the settlers, based on their fire-breathing capability and reptilian morphology) would protect the deep floors. The surface humans would maintain the upper halls. The Gleaner's Guild was founded to hold up the human side of the bargain.

The Compact is celebrated as a founding document. It is cited in civic ceremonies. The Admiralty's role in drafting it is acknowledged — they are, after all, the scholars of Coralshore.

### What Actually Happened

The Admiralty did not discover dragons in Coralshore. They placed them there.

Eighty years before the founding of Coralshore, the Admiralty's predecessor institution had been studying a class of long-lived chimeric organisms of unclear origin — beings that appeared in fragmentary records from multiple ancient civilizations, always described as protectors, always associated with sacred sites. The Admiralty recognized a pattern: these beings were bioengineered, not naturally evolved. Someone had made them, for a purpose, a very long time ago.

The Admiralty believed they could replicate the engineering from these ancient templates — reverse-engineering, not original creation. They were correct.

The Admiralty's "dragons" introduced to Coralshore were not wild creatures. They were purpose-bred specimens — chimeric beings with:
- Extreme longevity (400+ year natural lifespan without intervention)
- Complex social intelligence (capable of negotiating the Compact)
- Unique biological properties the Admiralty intended to study for centuries

The Dragon Compact was designed by Admiralty scholars as a legal framework that:
1. Granted the Admiralty access to the deep chambers under the guise of "scholarship"
2. Prevented other surface parties from entering the deep floors
3. Created a Guild (the Gleaners) to maintain a civilian buffer

The Admiralty embedded themselves as the Moonpetal Apothecary on Lantern Row — a legitimate medicinal and academic institution. Their real research happened in the **Lamplit Catacombs** beneath the building, accessible only through passages not shown on any Guild map.

For 320 years, this arrangement held. The Admiralty studied. The dragons lived. The Guild worked. No one asked questions.

## The Foundry Collective's Discovery and Infiltration

### The Discovery (80 Years Ago)

The Foundry Collective was, at the time, primarily a regional arms manufacturer and private intelligence firm — the Pinkertons of Coralshore. They were hired to investigate "unusual procurement patterns" at the Moonpetal Apothecary by a business rival. What their investigator found instead was the Lamplit Catacombs.

The field report — now known as the **Crow Predecessor Documents** — described the Catacombs in detail: preserved dragon specimens, tissue samples in sealed vials, detailed biological catalogs going back three centuries. The investigator understood immediately what the Admiralty had been doing.

The Foundry's leadership convened. They considered several options:
- **Expose the secret**: Destroys the Admiralty's credibility; disrupts the established order; no profit
- **Blackmail the Admiralty**: Short-term revenue; dangerous long-term
- **Participate in the supply chain**: Long-term revenue; scalable; Admiralty can't complain

They chose participation.

### The Infiltration Architecture

Within five years, the Foundry had established:

1. **Hero Licensing**: By quietly placing Foundry representatives on the Guild Oversight Board, they introduced a "hero licensing" program ostensibly to regulate adventuring for safety purposes. In practice, it gave the Foundry veto power over which heroes entered Coralshore dungeons — and therefore control over which "specimens" were targeted.

2. **Sponsorship Contracts**: The Foundry began sponsoring heroes directly. Sponsored heroes received premium gear, dungeon intelligence, and a "procurement rider" in their contract requiring them to report all significant organic finds. Most heroes read this as a standard recovery clause. It was a harvest contract.

3. **The Trade Publication**: *The Adventurer's Quarterly* was founded and funded by Foundry shadow accounts. It manufactured demand by presenting dragon-slaying as glamorous, patriotic, and lucrative. It ran sponsored "destination" features on Coralshore, positioning the town as the premier dragon-hunting location.

4. **Tide Council Integration**: The Tide Council — already in the artifact trade — was brought in as the laundering mechanism. Dragon remains went from hero to Foundry front buyer to Tide Council to Admiralty contract. Each step obscured the source.

### The System at Scale

After two generations, the system was self-sustaining. The Foundry no longer needed to push. Heroes arrived voluntarily. Artifact prices were publicly listed. The Tide Council operated openly. The Guild reset floors on a three-day cycle. The Admiralty received steady deliveries of "research material" through legitimate channels.

The only problem: the supply was running out. There were not many dragons to begin with. After 80 years of the hero trade, the number of surviving Compact-protected specimens was in the single digits. The Foundry's own harvest logs — if anyone were to find and analyze them — would show a clear depletion curve.

## The Necromancer's Fall and Pivot Strategy

### The Necromancer as Guardian

The Necromancer was the original Compact guardian — the entity who had negotiated the 400-year-old agreement on behalf of the dragons and facilitated the establishment of the Gleaner's Guild. The Necromancer is not human (or not conventionally so), extremely long-lived, and deeply invested in the survival of the dragons.

For the first 320 years of the Compact, the Necromancer operated openly — not in public, but in the sense that the Admiralty knew their role and the Guild's Oversight Board had a back-channel. The system worked. The Necromancer was a known actor in a known arrangement.

### The Discovery and the Failed Response

When the Necromancer discovered the Foundry's infiltration (approximately 60 years ago, after the system had been running for 20 years and the Necromancer noticed the harvest rate in the deep chambers), the options were constrained:

- **Open warfare**: The Foundry had Guild infiltration, political connections, and a legal licensing structure. Any open accusation could be deflected as a rogue actor's conspiracy theory.
- **Admiralty alliance**: The mainstream Admiralty was by this point so dependent on the Foundry's supply chain that they would not support disruption.
- **Father Ashworth faction**: Ashworth's internal Admiralty dissent was not yet organized.

The Necromancer chose misdirection.

### The Dungeon Reset Economy

The Necromancer approached the Guild's uninfiltrated layer (the Dispatcher and operational staff, not the Oversight Board) with a commercial proposal: standardize dungeon resets as a professional service. Offer Guild members a "dungeon maintenance" contract. The Necromancer would fund the contract through work orders.

This created several advantages:
1. **Cover**: Regular Guild operatives cycling through the dungeons provided plausible reason for the Necromancer's people to also be in the dungeons — maintaining the "reset" as cover for actually monitoring the deep chambers.
2. **Information**: Gleaner-class operatives, doing their jobs, generated foot traffic reports, inventory logs, and anomaly observations that gave the Necromancer real-time intelligence on hero activity without having to deploy a shadow network.
3. **Delay**: Every successful restock run extended the time before a hero could re-enter a cleared floor. Three-day reset cycles meant three fewer days of hunting per cleared dungeon. Small numbers, but the remaining dragons needed every day.

Gleaner — the player character — is the current generation of this cover operation. They don't know they are protecting anything. They think they're cleaning.

## The Admiralty's Internal Schism

### Father Ashworth's Position

Father Ashworth is one of the Admiralty's twelve inner-circle members. He has been in the Catacombs. He has seen the harvest rate. He has done the math.

Ashworth's position is simple: at current depletion rates, the last Compact-protected specimens will be dead within three hero cycles. The Admiralty's 400-year project — the study of the chimera beings, their biology, their origin — will end. Not because the project is complete, but because the subject matter will be extinct.

Ashworth's proposal: release the **Providence Codex**.

The Providence Codex is the Admiralty's own internal founding document — the one that predates the Dragon Compact, the one that describes what the "dragons" actually are, how they were made, and why the Admiralty brought them to Coralshore. If released, it would:
- Prove that the Dragon Compact was designed, not negotiated
- Prove that the "dragons" are sapient protectors, not monsters
- Expose the Foundry Collective's role as a systematic extermination operation
- End the hero trade in Coralshore (and likely trigger significant political fallout)
- Destroy the Admiralty's public credibility but preserve their actual scientific legacy

### The Mainstream's Position

The mainstream Admiralty — represented by the Oversight Board majority — does not disagree with Ashworth's math. They disagree with his conclusion.

Their position: the Codex release triggers retaliation from the Foundry Collective. The Tide Council loses their Admiralty contracts and becomes hostile. The Guild Oversight Board — already partially Foundry-infiltrated — moves to shut down Guild access to the Catacombs. The Admiralty loses 400 years of research infrastructure in the political fallout.

Their preference: continue quiet research as long as any specimens remain; accelerate the Ashworth faction's internal proposal to "re-introduce" a new generation of chimera beings once the current population is gone.

This position is morally indefensible. They know it. They have chosen it anyway.

### Dr. Yuen's Role

**Dr. Yuen** was an Admiralty field researcher who independently arrived at Ashworth's conclusions through a different route: she was cataloging longevity anomalies in the deep specimens and realized that the Catacombs were not a passive research facility but an active harvest operation. Specimens were not being *studied* and released. They were being studied until a harvest event, then the remains were transferred to the Tide Council procurement chain.

Dr. Yuen documented her findings, attempted to present them to the inner circle, was told her findings were "under review," and disappeared shortly afterward. Her research notes are scattered through the deep floors — left where she hoped someone else would find them.

Whether she is alive is left deliberately ambiguous through Act 1.

## The Industrialization of Adventuring as a Systemic Problem

The hero trade is not an aberration — it is the natural endpoint of the system the Foundry designed. The key insight is that the Foundry did not set out to commit genocide. They set out to profit. The genocide is a side effect they chose to ignore.

### The Self-Reinforcing Loop

```
Foundry manufactures demand (Adventurer's Quarterly, sponsored heroes)
→ Heroes enter dungeons seeking dragons
→ Heroes kill dragons; report finds to Foundry field reps
→ Foundry sells remains to Tide Council
→ Tide Council sells to Admiralty
→ Admiralty pays premium; demand signal goes back to Foundry
→ Foundry increases recruitment; hero pipeline grows
→ More heroes enter dungeons seeking dragons
```

The loop has no natural exit condition. It will terminate only when there are no more dragons to kill — at which point the Foundry will move to a new territory, or find a new product.

### The Moral Architecture

Each actor in the loop has a rationale:
- **The Foundry**: We're a commercial operation. We provide services. What happens to the product after delivery is not our department.
- **The Tide Council**: We are a market. We don't ask about provenance. Everyone in trade has a Tide Council equivalent.
- **The Admiralty (mainstream)**: We are scientists. We use what is available. If we don't use it, someone else will, less carefully.
- **The Heroes**: We are licensed. The Guild certifies our targets. We are hunting what we have been told is a monster.
- **The Guild Oversight Board**: We are an administrative body. We process applications. The licensing standards were set by a committee.
- **The Dispatcher**: We do maintenance scheduling. We don't have security clearance for the deep-floor contracts.

Every actor has plausible deniability. No single actor is responsible for the whole. This is the systemic horror: it is a machine for producing atrocities through distributed responsibility.

## The Tide Council's Moral Compromise

The Tide Council's position deserves special attention because it is the most human: they know, and they chose not to know.

Approximately 50 years into the trade, a Tide Council merchant — the historical record names them only as "the Third Factor" — discovered through a supplier's misdirected invoice that the "dragon artifacts" they were handling were from beings still alive at time of procurement. They raised the issue internally. The Council's response: reclassify the invoice categories, implement a new procurement ledger format that obscured the harvest timeline, and remind all factors that "provenance inquiries are conducted by the Admiralty's own review board."

The Third Factor left the Council shortly afterward. The Council continued.

The current Tide Council leadership knows this history. They work with it. The artifact trade represents 40% of their premium inventory revenue. The Admiralty contract is their largest single client. They have long-term contracts, embedded relationships, and a financial infrastructure built on this trade.

Their preferred narrative: "We are a market. We do not manufacture product. We do not source. We facilitate exchange. The ethics of production are the responsibility of the producers."

In the player's experience, this appears as: a cheerful vendor who keeps asking Gleaner if they've found anything "unusual" in the crates.

## Player Agency: Three Endings

### Ending 1 — "Standard Report" (Complicity)

Gleaner files the standard report. They continue their work. The dungeon economy continues. The Necromancer's cover is maintained. The last dragons gain more time — but the hero cycle continues. The system persists.

**Tone**: Not a failure state. The Necromancer needs operatives who ask no questions. Gleaner chose to be useful in the only way the system allows. The game acknowledges this without condemnation.

**Post-credits**: A work order arrives for next month's reset. Business as usual.

### Ending 2 — "Report the Anomaly" (Disruption)

Gleaner reports the anomaly to the Guild. The Oversight Board activates. The Foundry's Guild infiltrators move to contain the information. The Dispatcher is sidelined. Gleaner is reassigned. But the report exists in the Guild system — and Father Ashworth's faction gets a copy through back channels. Act 2 begins with Gleaner under surveillance and Ashworth's Providence Codex plan accelerating.

**Tone**: Gleaner has made themselves a piece on the board. The system is now aware of them.

### Ending 3 — "Contact Handler Vala" (Resistance)

Gleaner finds the cipher in the deep floors and decodes Vala's message. They respond. Vala reveals partial truth: the dragons are not monsters; the resets are a cover; the Necromancer is trying to protect what's left. Gleaner now has a choice — become a knowing operative in the cover, or try to find and release the Providence Codex directly.

**Tone**: The most dangerous path. The Foundry will mark Gleaner as a threat. The Seeker may become an unexpected ally. This is the Act 2 hook.

## Design Notes for Contributors

### On Writing In-Game Text

Every in-game document should be written by a **person who does not know they are in a conspiracy**. The Guild memo writer thinks they are writing a policy document. The Foundry field agent thinks their report is mundane operational paperwork. The Admiralty handbook author thinks their ethics guideline is benign.

The horror comes from the gap between the author's certainty and the reader's growing understanding.

### On the Dragons

The dragons must never be sentimentalized. They are ancient, strange, and not human. But they are also real actors — they negotiated the Compact, they maintain the deep chambers, they have been patient for 400 years. Their sapience should be felt through the precision and care of the deep chamber architecture — the same spaces the heroes trash and Gleaner cleans.

### On Gleaner

Gleaner has a voice. They are competent and a little smug. The field journals should start confident and gradually shift in register — not panicked, but recalibrating. The player should feel Gleaner is always one step behind the reader who has found all the books.

### On The Seeker

The Seeker is not a villain. They are a weapon pointed at a target they may or may not know is wrong. Their journal fragment is written with the same careful observation as Gleaner's — the difference is that The Seeker has access to the Foundry's intelligence and has started questioning it. In some paths, they are Gleaner's most important ally.

### On the Necromancer

The Necromancer is never seen. They communicate only through work orders. Contributors should resist the urge to clarify or explain the Necromancer. Their mysteriousness is structural — they are the only actor in the system who is not deceiving Gleaner, and the way to convey that is through the uncanny precision of their work orders, which always seem to put Gleaner in exactly the right place at the right time.
