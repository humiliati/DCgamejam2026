§1 — Premise: Dungeon Gleaner

You are a Nez-Ha — a sleek, modern scavenger-cleaner who follows in the devastating wake of a legendary Hero. The Hero storms through dungeons, slays monsters, smashes everything in sight, and vanishes before anyone can thank (or stop) him. You arrive after to pick through the wreckage, loot what's left, and deal with the twitching, gurgling remnants he didn't finish off.

The world is a small coastal boardwalk town — think Miami Vice meets a fantasy fishing village. Chrome railings on marble promenades. Sunset gradients on stone walls. Neon sigils over old timber shop fronts. The aesthetic is retrofuturistic fantasy: a civilization that discovered magic before technology, and their architecture shows it.

Three outdoor districts form the town. Buildings along each district are shops, inns, and guild halls. Beneath certain buildings, dungeons descend — already torn through by the Hero. Every dungeon is littered with defeated enemies (lootable corpses with serious fantasy names), environmental destruction, and the comedic living remnants the Hero didn't quite finish off (slime blobs, severed appendages, half-dead creatures).

Jam scope: 3 exterior districts, 5–6 building interiors, 3 dungeons (one per district with dungeon access), 1 boss encounter (the Hero, end of Act 1). Post-jam: Act 2 (dragon alliance), Act 3 (world map).
Nez-Ha universe connection: Dragons are ancient allies and protectors of the world. The Hero hunts them, believing they are monsters. The Nez-Ha (player) discovers through environmental clues and NPC dialogue that the Hero is wrong — the dragons were guardians. The conspiracy reveal at the end of Act 1: the Hero is either corrupted, misguided, or working for the true villain. End-of-jam climax: confront the Hero.

§3 — Renderer Fidelity & Retrofuturism Visual Philosophy

The raycaster renders flat-colored wall strips in the Wolfenstein 3D tradition. Each biome provides 6 colors (wallLight, wallDark, door, doorDark, ceil, floor) and the raycaster shades them by distance and facing. There are no texture maps, no sprite billboards, no per-wall-tile art.

What we CANNOT do: Photorealistic storefronts, neon sign rendering, or individual shop art. A potion shop and an armory are both just wall columns in a specific color. Buildings are communicated through DOOR tile placement and the dramatic palette shift when you enter a depth-2 interior.

What We CAN Do — Retrofuturism Through Color

  • Saturated palettes: The retrofuturism aesthetic lives in the color choices. Instead of muted realism, we lean into teal-and-magenta sunsets, chrome pinks, electric purples, and warm sunset oranges. Every biome palette should feel like a vaporwave album cover translated to Wolfenstein walls.
  • Fog as atmosphere: Exterior fog colors are sunset-tinted (warm peach, cool lavender). Interior fog is warmer (amber, rose). Dungeon fog goes deep (indigo, void purple). The fog gradient IS the retrofuturism.
  • Parallax as skyline: Background bands create the boardwalk horizon — distant water shimmer, sunset gradient bands, silhouetted palm trees (as color blocks). These set the Miami-Vice-meets-fantasy tone before the player sees a single wall.
  • Ceiling/floor as mood: Sunset-pink sky above the Promenade. Deep teal sky above the Frontier Gate. Polished marble floors in the Market. Rough stone in dungeons. These two colors carry enormous tonal weight.
  • The Hero's destruction as identity: Dungeons look different from pristine interiors because the Hero wrecked them. Dungeon palettes are desaturated, cracked, chaotic versions of their parent interior's palette — the same hues but darker, more broken. The player reads "this was beautiful before the Hero came through."
Design principle: Each district must be identifiable within 3 seconds of spawning by color + fog + parallax alone. The Promenade is warm sunset coral. The Lantern Gardens are cool teal-green. The Frontier Gate is deep magenta-purple. If a playtester can't tell them apart without reading a label, the palettes aren't different enough.

§4 — Floor ID Convention & Spatial Contracts

Every floor has a string ID that encodes its depth. The ID drives which SpatialContract constructor is used, which in turn controls the raycaster's fog model, wall height, ceiling type, render distance, and tile height offsets.

DepthID PatternExampleContractDescription
1"N""1" SpatialContract.exterior() Outdoor district. Sky ceiling. Fog FADE. 16–20 tile render distance. +0.15 door offset.

§5 — The Boardwalk Town (Depth 1)

Three exterior floors form the town of Coralshore — a small coastal boardwalk settlement built on ancient foundations. The retrofuturism aesthetic is strongest here: chrome-trimmed marble, sunset-gradient skies, neon sigil shop signs, palm shadows on polished stone. The player transitions between districts via DOOR tiles at grid edges.

Exterior layout is contrived, not proc-gen. Each district is a hand-authored grid (30×30). GridGen.generate() is NOT called for depth-1 floors. Instead, FloorManager loads a template from data/streets/. This is jam work.

§7 — Dungeons: The Hero's Mess (Depth 3)

These are the actual dungeon-crawl floors — the Hero's aftermath. Proc-gen via GridGen.generate(). SpatialContract.nestedDungeon() — 1x wall height, fog DARKNESS, void ceiling. Each dungeon is a wrecked version of what was once a beautiful or functional space. The Hero tore through, left destruction, and moved on.

Environmental Storytelling (every dungeon)

  • Hero's slash marks — specific wall tiles are "damaged" (dimmer palette variant). The Hero carved through walls.
  • Lootable corpses — CHEST tiles representing fallen enemies. Serious fantasy names (Fallen Wyrm Knight, Shattered Golem, Slain Cavern Drake). Loot = cards, gold, element shards.
  • Living remnants — the actual enemies the player fights. Comedic half-creatures the Hero didn't finish. They're angry, confused, and pathetic.
  • Rubble tiles — passable but slow. The Hero smashed through without caring about structural integrity.
  • Dragon clues — each dungeon has 1–2 environmental lore items (dragon scale, old mural fragment, grateful inscription from a past civilization) that build toward the Act 1 reveal.

§8 — Opening Scene & First Dungeon Scripted Sequence

The opening scene is the player's introduction to the "cleaning up the hero's mess" concept. It plays when the player first enters the Coral Cellars ("1.1.1").

Sequence

  1. Player arrives at Coral Cellars entrance. Descends via STAIRS_DN from the Coral Bazaar. Normal floor transition (fade to black, SFX, fade in).
  2. Fade-in reveals the Hero's back. The player spawns facing north. 3–5 tiles ahead, the Hero (a tall silhouette, rendered as a special wall column or HUD overlay) is visible. The Hero's arm swings — an enemy in front of him flashes and vanishes (death animation). A second enemy tries to flee; the Hero cuts it down.
  3. The Hero walks away. Without turning around, the Hero walks through a DOOR on the far wall and vanishes. The door slams shut behind him. The sound echoes.
  4. The dungeon is now the player's. Silence. Then: a wet squelching sound. A slime puddle to the left twitches. A disembodied claw scuttles across the floor. The remnants are waking up. A HUD message appears: "The Hero was here. Clean up what's left."
  5. Gameplay begins. The player can now explore, fight remnants, loot corpses, and find the first dragon clue.
Implementation: The Hero sequence is a one-time scripted event triggered by FloorManager when floorId === "1.1.1" and the opening_complete event flag is false. After it plays, the flag is set and the sequence never replays. The Hero is NOT an enemy entity — he's a visual overlay/cutscene element. Post-jam, this system generalizes for other Hero sightings.
Jam scope for the opening: At minimum, a text overlay sequence ("You see the Hero ahead..." / "He cuts down the last creature..." / "He vanishes through the far door." / "Something is still moving..."). Ideal: actual visual silhouette + animation. The text version ships if time runs out.

§9 — Boss Encounters & The Hero

No frogger/skifree encounters. Gone Rogue's boss arenas are top-down mechanics that don't translate to first-person. Bosses use the repo's existing chamberOverrides pattern in SpatialContract.

How Bosses Work in This Engine

  1. A chamberOverrides entry targets the last room (roomIndex: -1) and raises its wallHeight to 1.8x
  2. GridGen places a BOSS_DOOR tile (value 14) at the entrance to that room
  3. The raycaster renders the boss door with +0.15 tile height offset — visually prominent
  4. When the player walks through the BOSS_DOOR, CombatBridge triggers boss combat
  5. The boss room has taller walls, creating a cathedral-like arena in first person

The Hero as Boss (End of Act 1)

The Hero is encountered at the end of the Ironhold Depths ("3.1.1"). He's standing in the boss chamber, surrounded by the destruction he's caused. A dying dragon (environmental prop) is visible behind him. This is the first time the player sees the Hero's face.

§10 — Rock-Paper-Scissors Combat Triangle

Core mechanic: Every combat card and every enemy has one of three elements. Advantage deals 2x damage. Disadvantage deals 0.5x damage. Neutral deals 1x.

FLAME beats FROST  →  FROST beats STORM  →  STORM beats FLAME

Implementation

ComponentHow It Uses Elements
CardSystem Every card has an element field: 'flame', 'frost', 'storm', or 'neutral'. Neutral cards deal 1x regardless. Element is displayed as a colored icon on the card in the HUD (5-card hand). FLAME = orange border, FROST = blue border, STORM = purple border.
CombatEngine Damage multiplier lookup: getElementMultiplier(attackerElement, defenderElement). Returns 2.0, 1.0, or 0.5. Applied after base damage calculation, before defense. The multiplier is shown as a popup: EFFECTIVE! (2x) or RESISTED (0.5x).
EnemyAI Each enemy deck has a primary element. The enemy's nameplate shows its element badge. Players learn to read the element before engaging and pick the right cards. Enemies in the same dungeon tend toward one element, with variety enemies as curveballs.
HUD Enemy element badge: colored icon next to HP bar. Player hand: each card has element border. Damage popup: multiplier text. No complex UI — three colors tell the whole story.
SynergyEngine Playing 2+ cards of the same element in one combat triggers an Elemental Surge: +25% damage on the second same-element card. This rewards deck-building around a primary element while still needing coverage cards for disadvantage matchups.

Element Distribution by Dungeon

DungeonPrimary ElementVarietyRationale
Coral Cellars FLAME 90% flame remnants, 10% neutral Tutorial. Player starts with FROST cards. Easy advantage matchup teaches the system.
Lamplit Catacombs FROST 60% frost, 25% storm, 15% neutral Mid-game. Players need FLAME cards but also encounter STORM enemies that punish flame-heavy decks. Teaches deck diversity.
Ironhold Depths Mixed 33% flame, 33% frost, 33% storm Endgame. All elements in equal measure. Players must read enemy elements in real-time and pick cards carefully. Prepares them for the Hero (who uses all three).
