§1 — Premise: Dungeon Gleaner

You are a Nez-Ha — a dungeon janitor working for a Necromancer on the wrong side of a fantasy economy. Legendary Heroes storm through dungeons, slaughter monsters, smash everything, loot the place, and vanish. You arrive after to clean up the mess — scrubbing walls, restocking crates with potions and food, re-arming traps, reassembling dead monsters so the Necromancer can revive them, and scrambling puzzles back to their unsolved state. All so the dungeon can be reset and the next batch of adventurers can "discover" it. Think Power Wash Simulator meets Dungeon Keeper from a minimum-wage perspective.

The world is a small coastal boardwalk town — think Miami Vice meets a fantasy fishing village. Chrome railings on marble promenades. Sunset gradients on stone walls. Neon sigils over old timber shop fronts. The aesthetic is retrofuturistic fantasy: a civilization that discovered magic before technology, and their architecture shows it.

Three outdoor districts form the town. Buildings along each district are shops, inns, and guild halls. Beneath certain buildings, dungeons descend — already torn through by the Hero. Every dungeon is littered with defeated enemies (lootable corpses with serious fantasy names), environmental destruction, and the comedic living remnants the Hero didn't quite finish off (slime blobs, severed appendages, half-dead creatures).

Jam scope: 3 exterior districts, 5–6 building interiors, 3 dungeons (one per district). Core loops: grid-by-grid tile cleaning, crate slot restocking (Kingdom Two Crowns coin economy), and dungeon reset work orders. Heroes patrol as environmental hazards. Late-game Hero boss encounters using combat decks earned through labor. Post-jam: Act 2 (dragon alliance), Act 3 (world map), LG webOS TV port.
Nez-Ha universe connection: Dragons are ancient allies and protectors of the world. The Hero hunts them, believing they are monsters. The Nez-Ha (player) discovers through environmental clues and NPC dialogue that the Hero is wrong — the dragons were guardians. The conspiracy reveal at the end of Act 1: the Hero is either corrupted, misguided, or working for the true villain. End-of-jam climax: confront the Hero.

§2 — Theme Integration (All Four)

All four DC Jam 2026 themes are integrated. Only one is required; we use all four.

ThemeIntegrationWhere It Shows
Dragons Core lore. Dragons are ancient protectors being hunted by the Hero. The player gradually discovers this through environmental storytelling (dragon murals, grateful NPC memories, dragon-scale artifacts). The Hero's "heroism" is the conspiracy. Post-jam: the player allies with a dragon for Act 2. Dungeon environmental art (claw marks, scales on walls), lore items, NPC dialogue, final boss motivation, dragon-themed biome palettes in deeper dungeons
Retrofuturism The entire visual identity. The boardwalk town is a fantasy civilization that feels simultaneously ancient and futuristic — vaporwave sunsets, chrome-and-marble architecture, neon sigils, synthwave color palettes. Not pixel-art retro; conceptually retro — a future imagined by a magical past. Biome palettes (teal, magenta, sunset orange, chrome pink), wall color choices, fog tinting, parallax backgrounds, floor/ceiling gradients
Rock-Paper-Scissors Combat Suits Core combat mechanic. Playing card suits replace elemental naming: ♣ Clubs (Wild/Force) beats ♦ Diamonds (Crystal/Precision) beats ♠ Spades (Earth/Steel) beats ♣ Clubs. ♥ Hearts (Life/Blood) are neutral rule-breakers. Advantage = ×1.5 damage, disadvantage = ×0.75 damage. Each suit correlates to a resource color: ♠ free, ♣ energy, ♦ battery, ♥ HP. Combat is readable at a glance: suit color = cost color = advantage color. Card UI (suit glyph + resource-color border), enemy nameplate suit badge, HUD suit indicator, damage multiplier popup. Biome alignment: Cellar = ♠, Foundry = ♦, Sealab = ♣, cross-biome rare = ♥. See §10.
Cleaning Up the Hero's Mess The entire game — three interlocking loops. Cleaning: scrub dirty/damaged tiles grid-by-grid for coin rewards, progressive cleaning tools from Rag to Pressure Washer. Restocking: fill crate slots with matching items from shops, earn coins and rare combat cards on seal. Dungeon Reset: complete work orders (re-arm traps, scramble puzzles, reassemble monsters, restore secrets) to reach readiness thresholds. Combat cards are earned through labor, not combat — your deck is built crate by crate. The Skyrim dungeon keeper who refills the ancient crates with fresh cabbages? That's you. Dungeon layouts (dirty tiles, half-empty crates, disarmed traps, solved puzzles), Hero patrol routes (environmental hazard that undoes your work), crate slot UI (color-coded frames matching resource colors), work order board at Gleaner's Guild, cleaning texture variants (dirty/damaged/clean per tile)

§3 — Renderer Fidelity & Retrofuturism Visual Philosophy

The raycaster renders flat-colored wall strips in the Wolfenstein 3D tradition. Each biome provides 6 colors (wallLight, wallDark, door, doorDark, ceil, floor) and the raycaster shades them by distance and facing. There are no texture maps, no sprite billboards, no per-wall-tile art.

What we CANNOT do: Photorealistic storefronts, neon sign rendering, or individual shop art. A potion shop and an armory are both just wall columns in a specific color. Buildings are communicated through DOOR tile placement and the dramatic palette shift when you enter a depth-2 interior.

What We CAN Do — Retrofuturism Through Color

  • Saturated palettes: The retrofuturism aesthetic lives in the color choices. Instead of muted realism, we lean into teal-and-magenta sunsets, chrome pinks, electric purples, and warm sunset oranges. Every biome palette should feel like a vaporwave album cover translated to Wolfenstein walls.
  • Fog as atmosphere: Exterior fog colors are sunset-tinted (warm peach, cool lavender). Interior fog is warmer (amber, rose). Dungeon fog goes deep (indigo, void purple). The fog gradient IS the retrofuturism.
  • Parallax as skyline: Background bands create the boardwalk horizon — distant water shimmer, sunset gradient bands, silhouetted palm trees (as color blocks). These set the Miami-Vice-meets-fantasy tone before the player sees a single wall.
  • Ceiling/floor as mood: Sunset-pink sky above the Promenade. Deep teal sky above the Frontier Gate. Polished marble floors in the Market. Rough stone in dungeons. These two colors carry enormous tonal weight.
  • The Hero's destruction as identity: Dungeons look different from pristine interiors because the Hero wrecked them. Dungeon palettes are desaturated, cracked, chaotic versions of their parent interior's palette — the same hues but darker, more broken. The player reads "this was beautiful before the Hero came through."
Design principle: Each district must be identifiable within 3 seconds of spawning by color + fog + parallax alone. The Promenade is warm sunset coral. The Lantern Gardens are cool teal-green. The Frontier Gate is deep magenta-purple. If a playtester can't tell them apart without reading a label, the palettes aren't different enough.

§4 — Floor ID Convention & Spatial Contracts

Every floor has a string ID that encodes its depth. The ID drives which SpatialContract constructor is used, which in turn controls the raycaster's fog model, wall height, ceiling type, render distance, and tile height offsets.

DepthID PatternExampleContractDescription
1"N""1" SpatialContract.exterior() Outdoor district. Sky ceiling. Fog FADE. 16–20 tile render distance. +0.15 door offset.
2"N.N""1.1" SpatialContract.interior() Building interior (shop, inn, guild). 2x wall height. Fog CLAMP. Solid ceiling. Template-based layout.
3"N.N.N""1.1.1" SpatialContract.nestedDungeon() Dungeon (the Hero's mess). Proc-gen. Fog DARKNESS. Void ceiling. 1x wall height (with chamber overrides for boss rooms).

§5 — The Boardwalk Town (Depth 1)

Three exterior floors form the town of Coralshore — a small coastal boardwalk settlement built on ancient foundations. The retrofuturism aesthetic is strongest here: chrome-trimmed marble, sunset-gradient skies, neon sigil shop signs, palm shadows on polished stone. The player transitions between districts via DOOR tiles at grid edges.

Exterior layout is contrived, not proc-gen. Each district is a hand-authored grid (30×30). GridGen.generate() is NOT called for depth-1 floors. Instead, FloorManager loads a template from data/streets/. This is jam work.

§7 — Dungeons: The Hero's Mess (Depth 3)

These are the actual dungeon-crawl floors — the Hero's aftermath. Proc-gen via GridGen.generate(). SpatialContract.nestedDungeon() — 1x wall height, fog DARKNESS, void ceiling. Each dungeon is a wrecked version of what was once a beautiful or functional space. The Hero tore through, left destruction, and moved on.

Environmental Storytelling (every dungeon)

  • Hero's slash marks — specific wall tiles are "damaged" (dimmer palette variant). The Hero carved through walls.
  • Lootable corpses — CHEST tiles representing fallen enemies. Serious fantasy names (Fallen Wyrm Knight, Shattered Golem, Slain Cavern Drake). Loot = cards, gold, suit crystals.
  • Living remnants — the actual enemies the player fights. Comedic half-creatures the Hero didn't finish. They're angry, confused, and pathetic.
  • Rubble tiles — passable but slow. The Hero smashed through without caring about structural integrity.
  • Dragon clues — each dungeon has 1–2 environmental lore items (dragon scale, old mural fragment, grateful inscription from a past civilization) that build toward the Act 1 reveal.

§8 — Opening Scene & First Dungeon Scripted Sequence

The opening scene introduces both the narrative hook and the janitor role. It plays when the player first enters Cellar B1 ("0.1.1"). The Hero's wake — CORPSE tiles, DEBRIS, scattered COLLECTIBLE pickups, blood spatter on floor textures — is pre-placed during floor generation. This is the player's first encounter with the mess they'll spend the rest of the game cleaning.

Sequence

  1. Descent transition. TransitionFX plays the descend preset. Label: "Cellar B1".
  2. Spawn & Hero reveal. Player appears at STAIRS_UP, facing into the corridor. The Hero sprite is visible ~8 tiles ahead — a towering figure (scale 1.5, gold glow, particle trail) walking away through a corridor littered with corpses and debris. Dialog auto-triggers after 1.5s: "Something massive came through here. The walls are gouged, the floor is littered with… remains. A figure moves in the darkness ahead."
  3. Player gains control. Can loot corpses (3–5 CORPSE tiles yield gold + chance of a common card), collect walk-over pickups (gold, food), or chase the Hero.
  4. Hero movement. Hero walks at 800ms/tile along a pre-scripted path. When player closes to within 4 tiles, Hero speeds up to 500ms/tile. When Hero reaches the corner room, it despawns. A faint ascend-3 SFX plays (the Hero went deeper).
  5. Interceptor triggers. When player passes a side room (y-coordinate check), the Wounded Shambler (HP 4, STR 2, pre-set high awareness) steps out. CombatBridge.startCombat() fires. First combat with 5-card starter deck. Tight but winnable (player ends at 6–8 HP).
  6. Post-combat dialog. "That thing was already half-dead. Whatever came through here… you don't want to catch up to it."
  7. First cleaning opportunity. The dungeon is dirty. Scrub a wall tile — coin pops out. Find a half-empty crate — fill its slots or smash it. The maintenance loop begins here, interwoven with the scavenge loop from the original design.
Implementation: The Hero sequence is a one-time scripted event triggered by FloorManager when floorId === "0.1.1" and the opening_complete event flag is false.
Jam scope for the opening: At minimum, a text overlay sequence ("You see the Hero ahead..." / "He cuts down the last creature..." / "He vanishes through the far door." / "Something is still moving..."). Ideal: actual visual silhouette + animation. The text version ships if time runs out.

§9 — Boss Encounters & The Hero

No frogger/skifree encounters. Gone Rogue's boss arenas are top-down mechanics that don't translate to first-person. Bosses use the repo's existing chamberOverrides pattern in SpatialContract.

How Bosses Work in This Engine

  1. A chamberOverrides entry targets the last room (roomIndex: -1) and raises its wallHeight to 1.8x
  2. GridGen places a BOSS_DOOR tile (value 14) at the entrance to that room
  3. The raycaster renders the boss door with +0.15 tile height offset — visually prominent
  4. When the player walks through the BOSS_DOOR, CombatBridge triggers boss combat
  5. The boss room has taller walls, creating a cathedral-like arena in first person

The Hero as Boss (End of Act 1)

The Hero is encountered at the end of the Ironhold Depths ("3.1.1"). He's standing in the boss chamber, surrounded by the destruction he's caused. A dying dragon (environmental prop) is visible behind him. This is the first time the player sees the Hero's face.

§10 — Rock-Paper-Scissors Combat Suits

Core mechanic: Playing card suits drive the RPS advantage triangle. Every combat card and every enemy has a suit. Advantage deals ×1.5 damage. Disadvantage deals ×0.75 damage. Neutral (same suit or ♥) deals ×1.0.

♣ Clubs (Wild/Force) beats ♦ Diamonds (Crystal/Precision)  →  ♦ Diamonds beats ♠ Spades (Earth/Steel)  →  ♠ Spades beats ♣ Clubs

♥ Hearts (Life/Blood) — outside the triangle. No direct advantage. Rule-breaker suit: strong vs status effects, weak vs burst damage.

§17 — Gleaner Maintenance Systems (Pivot)

The Gleaner pivot expands the Act 1 scavenging period into the core game loop. Three interlocking systems drive the experience. All three yield coins (the universal currency, Kingdom Two Crowns style) and occasional combat cards (the endgame payoff). The player builds their combat deck entirely through labor.

17.1 — The Cleaning Loop (Grid-by-Grid)

Every wall and floor tile has a condition state: pristine, dirty, damaged, destroyed. Heroes leave the dungeon in dirty/damaged state. The Gleaner scrubs tiles grid-by-grid.

InteractionInputDurationCoin YieldNoise
Scrub (dirty → clean)[F] or pointer~400ms1 coin0.3
Repair (damaged → dirty)[F] or pointer~400ms0 (must then scrub)0.5

Texture system: texture-atlas.js generates 64×64 wall textures. Each base texture gets 3 variants via procedural noise overlay: _clean (default, vibrant retrowave palette), _dirty (desaturated, grime noise), _damaged (cracked, rubble overlay). The clean→dirty shift is a saturation drop — the vaporwave colors literally fade until you scrub them back.

Floor cleanliness: FloorStateTracker tracks (clean + pristine) / total per floor. Reaching 80% triggers a bonus coin burst (10–20 coins) and contributes to the floor's readiness score.

Crates and other containers have item slots that the player fills. Two interaction states toggled via the Gleaner's Apron equip:

Slot Anatomy

  • Each crate: 2–5 slots (avg 3). Rolled at floor generation.
  • Each slot: a frame tag (visual hint for ideal item category). Frame border uses canonical RESOURCE_COLORs: ♥ hp_food, △ energy_food, ◈ battery, 🂠 scroll, ¢ gem, wildcard.
  • Natural hydration: filledSlots = floor(totalSlots × random(0.3, 0.7)). A 4-slot crate might spawn 1–2 pre-filled, 2–3 empty.
  • ANY item fills ANY slot. Frame match is a bonus, not a gate.

17.3 — The Dungeon Reset Loop (Readiness Score)

Each dungeon floor has a readiness score (0–100%). The Gleaner's Guild issues work orders with minimum readiness thresholds. Readiness is a weighted sum:

TaskWeightInteraction
Tile cleanliness25%Scrub dirty/damaged tiles
Crates restocked25%Seal all crate slots
Traps re-armed15%Place trap component from bag into disarmed mechanism
Puzzles scrambled15%Interact with solved puzzle → "Scramble" → new unsolved state
Monsters reassembled10%Collect corpse parts (via salvage) → deposit at Reassembly Altar
Secrets restored10%Interact with exposed secret → "Conceal" → wall texture restored
The comedy of maintenance: You're literally re-hiding secrets that adventurers will "discover" again. You're scrambling puzzles that were supposed to be ancient riddles. You're putting fresh cabbages in crates that are supposed to be untouched for centuries. The entire job is maintaining the illusion of a pristine, ancient dungeon — and the Heroes keep wrecking it.

The player must hide from Heroes. Heroes use an enhanced version of the existing enemy-ai.js sight/awareness system:

Detection States

StateTriggerHero Behavior

EngagedPlayer adjacentForced interaction (see 18.4)

Hiding Mechanics

  • Side corridors: Step into a perpendicular corridor to break LOS. Hero passes by.
  • Behind breakables: Unbroken crate/barrel blocks LOS. Crouch behind it (hold crouch key). Hero can't see you, but if he smashes the crate…
  • Dark tiles: Tiles with low light value (from lighting.js) reduce detection range by 50%.
  • Clean vs dirty rooms: Heroes move faster through clean areas (don't stop to investigate). Dirty rooms slow Heroes down (they search for loot). Strategic tension: cleaning makes your job easier but makes Heroes faster on patrol.
  • Noise: Cleaning generates low noise (0.3). Restocking generates medium noise (0.8). Pressure Washer = 2.0. Hero responds to noise within 8 tiles: turns, pauses 2s.

Mid game10–19 cards Suspicious. "You? Fighting? With those cards?" Confiscates 1 random common card from deck. Non-lethal but punishing.

The power inversion: Early game, the Hero is an unstoppable force and you're invisible. Mid game, you're an annoyance. Late game, the janitor has become the dungeon's true defender — and the Hero discovers that every crate he smashes, every puzzle he solves, was restocked by someone who's been building a deck this whole time.

§19 — Faction Economy & Narrative Depth

The original STREET_CHRONICLES_NARRATIVE_OUTLINE.md described a multi-faction conspiracy with competing agendas. The Gleaner pivot amplifies this: the player is a minimum-wage worker navigating faction politics while trying to earn a living. Every shop, every NPC, and every dungeon reset benefits a faction — and no faction is clean.

19.1 — The Three Factions

Adapted from the Street Chronicles outline's faction structure. Each faction operates a shop chain, controls dungeon territory, and has an agenda the player gradually uncovers.

FactionShopTerritorySuit AlignmentPublic AgendaHidden Agenda
Tide Council Coral Bazaar ("1.1") Coral Cellars ♠ Spade Coastal trade, dungeon economy, adventurer supplies Harvesting dragon artifacts from the dungeons. Selling to an unseen buyer. They know the dragons were protectors — they don't care.
Foundry Collective Ironhold Armory ("3.1") Ironhold Depths ♦ Diamond Weapons, armor, combat training, mercenary contracts Arming the Hero deliberately. They profit from dungeon destruction — more wreckage = more demand for weapons and rebuild contracts. They don't want the dungeons to stay clean.
Admiralty Moonpetal Apothecary ("2.2") Lamplit Catacombs ♣ Club Potions, medicines, scholarly research, historical preservation Studying dragon remains for forbidden research. The catacombs are a laboratory, not a cemetery. They need the Gleaner to keep bringing them "samples."

19.2 — Reputation Economy

The existing shop.js faction reputation system drives prices. The Gleaner pivot adds faction-specific work orders and crate frame biases:

MechanicEffect
Sell to matching faction×1.10 price bonus (existing faction_sell_bonus in loot-tables.json)
Complete faction work order+reputation, unlock deeper shop inventory tiers
Restock crates in faction territorySlow reputation gain with the controlling faction
Restock with faction-sold itemsHigher coin yields (frame matching uses faction items as ideal matches)
High reputation (tier 3+)Faction NPCs reveal hidden agenda dialogue. Conspiracy unravels.
The economy trap: The player naturally gravitates toward one faction's shop for restock supplies (it's convenient, the items match crate frames in that faction's dungeon). But raising one faction's reputation reveals their crimes. The player realizes they've been complicit. This is the Street Chronicles moral weight translated to a work sim: every paycheck comes from someone dirty.

19.3 — The Necromancer (Your Employer)

The Necromancer is the player's employer — the one who pays for dungeon resets. They are never seen directly (voice through work order notes, occasional dialog at Reassembly Altars). Their agenda:

  • They need the dungeons reset because the Hero cycle generates profit. Adventurers pay to enter "ancient" dungeons.
  • They know the dragons were protectors. The Necromancer was the dragons' ally before the Hero arrived.
  • They revive monsters at Reassembly Altars not because they're evil — but because the dungeon ecosystem needs its inhabitants to function.
  • The Necromancer's eventual ask: stop the Hero. The late-game boss fights aren't just the player deciding to fight back — the Necromancer has been training you this whole time through labor. Every crate restocked was a card earned. Every monster reassembled was practice for the real fight.

19.4 — The Dragon Conspiracy (Adapted from Street Chronicles)

The original Street Chronicles outline featured a multi-layer conspiracy (pandas as chimeras, Jesuits covering up organ harvesting, multiple intelligence agencies at cross-purposes). The Gleaner adaptation preserves the structural complexity while rethemeing to fantasy:

Street ChroniclesDungeon GleanerNarrative Function
Chinese MSS (The Seeker) The Hero (The Seeker) Antagonist who believes they're the protagonist. Hunts dragons thinking they're evil. Discovers truth late (or never).
Pinkertons (ancient grudge) Foundry Collective (war profiteers) Saboteurs who benefit from chaos. Arm the Hero to keep the cycle going. "Accidentally" expose the truth through their greed.
Jesuits (chimera creators) Admiralty (forbidden researchers) Keepers of the original secret. Created the dungeon ecosystem. Study dragon remains. Need the cover-up to continue.
Homeland Security (player's employer) The Necromancer (player's employer) Assigns the player. Has their own agenda. Eventually asks player to choose sides.
Panda chimeras (MacGuffin) Dragons (ancient protectors) The truth everyone is hiding or hunting. Not monsters. Not weapons. Guardians.

19.5 — Story Arcs (Economy-Gated)

Progression is gated by economic milestones, not combat gates. The player advances by earning enough to unlock deeper work orders and faction reputation tiers.

ArcGateNarrative BeatFaction Reveal
Act 1: The Job First 3 work orders complete Learn the cleaning/restocking loops. First Hero encounter (humiliation). Build first 10 cards. Tide Council seems helpful. Foundry is aggressive. Admiralty is mysterious.
Act 2: The Routine Faction rep tier 2 with any faction Master the dungeon reset loop. Handle Hero patrols. Access deeper dungeons. Dragon clues accumulate. Chosen faction reveals first hint of hidden agenda. Other factions grow suspicious of player.
Act 3: The Truth Faction rep tier 3 + 20 cards Dragon conspiracy fully revealed. Necromancer asks player to stop the Hero. All factions converge. Player must choose: comply with employer, defect to a faction, or expose everyone.
Jam scope: Act 1 only. The faction economy and conspiracy reveals are post-jam content. For the jam, factions are shops with flavor text. The depth comes after.
